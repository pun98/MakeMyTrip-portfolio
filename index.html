The note nobody asked me to write

Cold Start

A partner's first 30 days, and the activation problem I'd want to own at Connect.

A note, not a pitch. I don't have Connect's data, so nothing here is a conclusion — it's a hypothesis, written the way I'd write one on my first day, to show how I'd think before I'd earned the right to an opinion.
1

The problem I'd look at first

Getting a hotel to sign isn't the win. A listing that's live, sellable, and priced right is the win. Everything between the signature and that first confirmed booking is a funnel — and like every funnel I've worked on, I'd bet it leaks in places nobody expects.

2

Who hurts — and why it's a marketplace problem

The independent property

18 rooms, no revenue manager, first time online. Wants heads in beds, not a console — and has a front desk to run.

The marketplace

Liquidity in the long tail is the moat. A signed partner who never goes live is dead liquidity — growth on a slide, nothing in the pool a traveler searches.

The traveler

Sees thinner supply and worse prices in exactly the long-tail segment the platform should win.

3

The hypothesis

Most first-30-day drop-off isn't unwilling partners. It's a setup process that asks for too much before the partner has felt a single rupee of value. (At PolicyBazaar, the funnel leaked for the same reason — we asked buyers for too much, too early.)

4

What I'd instrument before touching anything

  • Time-to-first-live-listing
  • Time-to-first-booking
  • % of signed partners who never go live — the leak that matters most
  • Setup-funnel drop-off, step by step
  • Parity & availability completeness at go-live

You don't fix a funnel you haven't measured.

5

The first thing I'd try — narrow, on purpose

Not a redesign. A "go-live in one sitting" path: the minimum that makes a listing sellable today, everything optional pushed to after the first booking. Ship to one segment, one city. Watch the go-live rate.

6

How I'd know it worked — and when I'd kill it

Win: time-to-first-live-listing down, go-live rate up, no rise in thin or parity-broken listings. Kill: if faster setup just produces listings nobody books. Fast onboarding that creates dead inventory is worse than slow onboarding that creates good inventory.

7

What I'm probably wrong about

Maybe the bottleneck is human — the field team, not the tool. Maybe go-live isn't the constraint; quality at go-live is. Maybe chains matter more than the long tail right now. I'd want the data before I'd defend any of this. But this is the shape of how I'd start.

Same problem I solved at PolicyBazaar, in a different costume: find where intent dies, subtract before you add, instrument the why. → The Activation Engine

Field Notes · from the window seat

Things I think about, written down.

Short notes on marketplaces, trust, and supply. Three, written properly, instead of twelve written thin.

Two-sided marketplaces are weakest the day they open. Buyers won't come without supply; supply won't stay without buyers. Everyone knows this in the abstract and underestimates it in the specific — because cold start isn't one problem, it's a new one in every city, every category, every segment of the long tail.

Insurance taught me you don't solve it by being clever on both sides at once. You pick the side that's cheaper to seed and over-invest there until the other has a reason to show up. At PolicyBazaar the scarce side wasn't demand — people wanted insurance — it was completed intent. So we stopped chasing more starts and removed reasons to abandon. The marketplace version is supply you can actually sell: not more listings, but more live, priced, bookable ones where travelers already search.

The trap is treating a sign-up as liquidity. A signed partner who never goes live adds nothing to the pool a traveler searches. It looks like growth on a slide and behaves like a dead listing in the funnel.

A marketplace asks two strangers to transact on its word. The traveler trusts the room exists, the photos are honest, the price won't move. The partner trusts the booking is real, the payout will come, the platform won't quietly compete with them. None of that trust is natural. All of it is manufactured — reviews, ratings, guarantees, verified badges, payout reliability. Trust is the product; rooms are just inventory.

And trust decays. A review system that worked last year gets gamed this year. You maintain it like infrastructure, against constant pressure from people trying to exploit it.

I learned a small version building a support model people had to believe. Accuracy got us in the room; what kept users was the model knowing when not to answer. A confident wrong answer cost more trust than ten honest "I'm not sure"s.

Acquisition is loud and retention is fashionable, so activation — the unglamorous middle — gets skipped. Which is strange, because it's usually where the value leaks. The sign-up isn't the win. The first time the user feels what they came for is the win, and everything between is a funnel nobody instrumented.

For a buyer, activation is the first purchase. For a hotel partner, it's the first live, sellable listing — better, the first confirmed booking. A platform that counts sign-ups measures a promise. One that counts time-to-first-value measures whether the promise was kept.
